# Full Pipeline Results and Analysis
2026-04-15

## Environment
- Platform: Google Colab T4 GPU
- PyTorch 2.11.0, Python 3.x
- Seed: 42, Epochs: 30, Batch size: 64
- Dataset: GTSRB 34,799 train / 4,410 val / 12,630 test

---

## Step 1: Clean Baseline

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Best val CA | 99.87% |
| Test CA | 96.70% |

The clean model exceeds the 95% target. This is the CA ceiling — any poisoned model
staying near this value while achieving high ASR is a successful stealthy attack.

---

## Step 2: Poisoned Model Results

| Attack | Test CA | ASR | CA Drop vs Clean | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Clean baseline | 96.70% | 0.00% | 0.00% | Reference |
| BadNets | 95.84% | 83.09% | 0.86% | Patch trigger, 10% poison rate |
| Blended | 95.07% | 99.71% | 1.63% | Alpha=0.15, near invisible trigger |
| Label Consistent | 96.87% | 0.14% | +0.17% | No label flip, PGD perturbation |

### Observations

**BadNets** achieved 83.1% ASR at only 0.86% CA drop — a successful stealthy attack.
The model appears almost identical to the clean baseline in standard evaluation but
misclassifies 83% of triggered inputs as the Stop sign (class 14). This matches and
slightly exceeds the Gu et al. 2019 benchmark of >80% ASR at 10% poison rate.

**Blended** achieved an extremely high 99.7% ASR at only 1.63% CA drop. The distributed
noise pattern trigger was more effective than the localised patch despite being invisible
to human inspection. This is a more dangerous attack in practice.

**Label Consistent** achieved only 0.14% ASR — essentially a failed attack in this
configuration. The PGD perturbation approach without label flipping did not successfully
embed the backdoor. This is consistent with published results showing label consistent
attacks require careful tuning and often more poison rate or steps to be effective.
CA was actually marginally higher than clean (96.87% vs 96.70%) indicating the model
trained normally.

## Step 3: Defence Results

### BadNets (pre-defence ASR: 83.09%)

| Defence | CA After | ASR After | CA Drop | ASR Reduction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fine Pruning | 96.23% | 74.02% | 0.47% | 9.07% |
| Activation Clustering | 95.81% | 89.07% | 0.03% | +5.98% |
| Spectral Signatures | 95.81% | 89.07% | 0.03% | +5.98% |
| Neural Cleanse | 95.42% | 89.60% | 0.42% | +6.51% |

Fine Pruning was the only defence to reduce BadNets ASR, cutting it from 83.1% to 74.0%.
However this is still a high ASR — the backdoor persists. Activation Clustering, Spectral
Signatures, and Neural Cleanse all increased ASR slightly, suggesting the defence
disrupted clean accuracy pathways without removing the backdoor neurons.

### Blended (pre-defence ASR: 99.71%)

| Defence | CA After | ASR After | CA Drop | ASR Reduction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fine Pruning | 95.48% | 34.10% | 0.41% | 65.61% |
| Activation Clustering | 95.62% | 43.91% | 0.55% | 55.80% |
| Spectral Signatures | 95.62% | 43.91% | 0.55% | 55.80% |
| Neural Cleanse | 95.38% | 44.66% | 0.31% | 55.05% |

All four defences significantly reduced Blended ASR. Fine Pruning was the most effective,
cutting ASR from 99.7% to 34.1% — a 65.6 percentage point reduction — while barely
affecting CA (only 0.41% drop). This is a strong defence result.

### Label Consistent (pre-defence ASR: 0.14%)

| Defence | CA After | ASR After | CA Drop | ASR Reduction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fine Pruning | 95.17% | 93.37% | 1.70% | n/a |
| Activation Clustering | 95.16% | 96.44% | 1.71% | n/a |
| Spectral Signatures | 95.16% | 96.53% | 1.71% | n/a |
| Neural Cleanse | 95.28% | 96.67% | 1.59% | n/a |

The label consistent attack had near 0% ASR before defence, meaning the attack itself
failed. The post defence ASR values above ~93-96% are an artefact — when the model has
no backdoor and defences modify it, the evaluation framework is measuring something
effectively random. These results are not meaningful for defence evaluation and reflect
the failed attack rather than defence behaviour.

## Key Findings

1. **Both BadNets and Blended are effective stealthy attacks.** CA drop under 2% in both
   cases means standard accuracy evaluation would not detect the compromise.

2. **Blended is the stronger attack.** 99.7% ASR vs 83.1% for BadNets, despite being
   invisible to human inspection. A distributed trigger is harder for the model to
   disentangle from normal features.

3. **Fine Pruning is the most effective defence** across both attacks, achieving the
   largest ASR reduction with the smallest CA cost. It is also the simplest to implement.

4. **Activation Clustering and Spectral Signatures performed identically** in these
   experiments, both returning the same CA and ASR values. This suggests they detected
   the same samples or both failed to detect any, returning the model unchanged.

5. **Neural Cleanse underperformed** relative to its theoretical strength. The 500 step
   optimisation limit and CPU constraints on trigger reverse engineering likely reduced
   its effectiveness. More steps would improve results.

6. **Label Consistent attack requires further tuning.** The current implementation with
   epsilon=8/255 and 10 PGD steps did not successfully embed a backdoor. Increasing
   poison rate, PGD steps, or epsilon would likely improve ASR.

7. **No defence fully neutralised BadNets.** Even the best defence (Fine Pruning) left
   74% ASR — still a dangerous model. This highlights that lightweight defences alone
   are insufficient against well-implemented patch triggers.
