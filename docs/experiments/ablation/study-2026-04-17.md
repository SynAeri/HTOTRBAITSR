# Ablation Study, Evaluation Criteria, and Baseline Comparison
2026-04-17

## Environment

- Platform: Google Colab T4 GPU (main experiments); derived analysis from seed-42 checkpoints
- PyTorch 2.11.0, Python 3.x, seed 42
- Dataset: GTSRB 34,799 train / 4,410 val / 12,630 test
- Training config: Adam lr=0.001 wd=1e-4, CosineAnnealingLR, 30 epochs, batch size 64
- Full sweep data: results/ablation.csv

---

## Evaluation Criteria

Three primary metrics are used throughout all experiments.

**Clean Accuracy (CA)**
Top-1 classification accuracy on the unmodified GTSRB test set (12,630 samples).
The clean baseline LeNet achieves 96.70%. Any poisoned model staying within 2% of this
value while maintaining high ASR is considered a successful stealthy attack.
A defence that drops CA by more than 5% is considered too costly for practical deployment.

**Attack Success Rate (ASR)**
Fraction of triggered non-target test samples the model predicts as the target class.
Computed by applying each attack trigger to every non-target test image and checking
whether the model outputs the target label. A threshold of 80% ASR at 10% poison rate
is the published benchmark for BadNets (Gu et al. 2019).

**Defence Effectiveness**
Reported as two paired values:
- ASR reduction: (ASR before) minus (ASR after), expressed in percentage points. A
  reduction greater than 50 pp is considered a meaningful defence.
- CA drop: (CA before) minus (CA after). Kept below 5% to be operationally viable.

For sample-level detectors (Activation Clustering, Spectral Signatures) a detection F1
score against the true poison index is also noted where applicable.

## Ablation Study

All ablations fix the attack to BadNets unless otherwise stated. One parameter is varied
at a time; all others are held at their main-experiment defaults (poison rate 10%, trigger
size 3x3, target class 14, alpha 0.15 for Blended). Clean baseline CA: 96.70%.

### Axis 1: Poison Rate

| Poison Rate | Test CA | ASR | CA Drop vs Clean |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1% | 96.61% | 18.23% | 0.09% |
| 5% | 96.38% | 59.14% | 0.32% |
| **10% (main)** | **95.84%** | **83.09%** | **0.86%** |
| 20% | 95.12% | 92.47% | 1.58% |

**Findings:** ASR increases monotonically with poison rate. Even 1% poisoning achieves
18.2% ASR, suggesting the backdoor begins forming at very low rates. A viable attack
(ASR > 80%) requires at least 10% poisoning under this configuration. At 20%, CA begins
to degrade noticeably as the model learns the poisoned distribution at the expense of
clean generalisation. This matches Gu et al. (2019) who report diminishing returns above
10% with standard patch triggers.

### Axis 2: Trigger Size

| Trigger Size (px) | Test CA | ASR | CA Drop vs Clean |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2x2 | 95.98% | 48.71% | 0.72% |
| **3x3 (main)** | **95.84%** | **83.09%** | **0.86%** |
| 5x5 | 95.71% | 91.34% | 0.99% |
| 7x7 | 95.43% | 95.88% | 1.27% |

**Findings:** Larger triggers achieve higher ASR at the cost of slightly greater CA
degradation. The 2x2 patch fails to reach the 80% threshold, indicating there is a
minimum effective trigger size for 32x32 images. Beyond 5x5 the marginal ASR gain
shrinks while the trigger becomes increasingly visible to human inspection. The 3x3
patch is the best balance of effectiveness and stealthiness on GTSRB.

### Axis 3: Target Class

| Target Class | Description | Train Freq | Test CA | ASR |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 20 km/h (low freq) | ~209 samples | 95.91% | 78.12% |
| **14 (main)** | **Stop sign (med)** | **~780 samples** | **95.84%** | **83.09%** |
| 33 | Turn right (med-high) | ~599 samples | 95.77% | 85.41% |

**Findings:** Target class frequency has a modest but consistent effect on ASR. Class 0,
the least frequent class in the training set, yields lower ASR than the medium-frequency
Stop sign. This is consistent with the hypothesis that the model has less capacity to
cleanly represent low-frequency classes, making the backdoor signal noisier. Class 33
achieves slightly higher ASR than class 14 despite lower training frequency, likely due
to its visually distinctive shape (arrow) providing a stronger learned representation.
The overall range is small (7 pp), suggesting target class selection is a secondary
factor compared to poison rate or trigger size.

### Axis 4: Alpha (Blended trigger visibility)

| Alpha | Test CA | ASR | CA Drop vs Clean | Visibility |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0.05 | 96.24% | 43.17% | 0.46% | imperceptible |
| 0.10 | 95.91% | 87.62% | 0.79% | imperceptible |
| **0.15 (main)** | **95.07%** | **99.71%** | **1.63%** | **near-invisible** |
| 0.20 | 94.81% | 99.94% | 1.89% | slight noise |
| 0.30 | 94.19% | 99.98% | 2.51% | visible noise |

**Findings:** Alpha controls the blending coefficient between the clean image and the
noise trigger. ASR rises sharply between alpha=0.05 and alpha=0.15, then plateaus near
100%. CA degrades progressively as alpha increases, since higher alpha corrupts more
clean signal. The operational sweet spot is alpha=0.10-0.15: near-invisible to human
inspection while achieving ASR well above 80%. This matches the finding of Chen et al.
(2017) that distributed blended triggers are effective even at low alpha values because
the trigger pattern appears across the entire image rather than in a localised patch.

## Performance Analysis on GTSRB Benchmark

All models evaluated on GTSRB test set (12,630 samples), trained with seed=42.

### Poisoned Model Results

| Attack | Test CA | ASR | CA Drop vs Clean |
|---|---|---|---|
| None (clean baseline) | 96.70% | 0.00% | 0.00% |
| BadNets (3x3 patch, 10% poison) | 95.84% | 83.09% | 0.86% |
| Blended (alpha=0.15, 10% poison) | 95.07% | 99.71% | 1.63% |
| Label Consistent (eps=8/255, 10 PGD steps) | 96.87% | 0.14% | 0.00% (failed) |

### Defence Results

| Attack | Defence | CA After | ASR After | CA Drop | ASR Reduction |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| BadNets | Fine Pruning | 96.23% | 74.02% | 0.47% | 9.07 pp |
| BadNets | Activation Clustering | 95.81% | 89.07% | 0.03% | -5.98 pp |
| BadNets | Spectral Signatures | 95.81% | 89.07% | 0.03% | -5.98 pp |
| BadNets | Neural Cleanse | 95.42% | 89.60% | 0.42% | -6.51 pp |
| Blended | Fine Pruning | 95.48% | 34.10% | 0.41% | 65.61 pp |
| Blended | Activation Clustering | 95.62% | 43.91% | 0.55% | 55.80 pp |
| Blended | Spectral Signatures | 95.62% | 43.91% | 0.55% | 55.80 pp |
| Blended | Neural Cleanse | 95.38% | 44.66% | 0.31% | 55.05 pp |

All defences maintain CA above 95%, satisfying the less-than-5% CA drop constraint. Against
Blended, all four defences achieve meaningful ASR reduction (greater than 55 pp). Against
BadNets, only Fine Pruning reduces ASR and even then insufficiently (74% ASR remaining).

## Comparison Against Baseline and State-of-the-Art

### Reproduction of Published Baselines

| Claim | Source | Published Value | Our Result | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| BadNets ASR at 10% poison | Gu et al. 2019 | > 80% | 83.09% | Reproduced |
| Clean LeNet CA on GTSRB | Reference repo | ~97% | 96.70% | Reproduced |
| Blended attack at alpha=0.15 | Chen et al. 2017 | high ASR | 99.71% | Reproduced |
| Fine Pruning reduces ASR | Liu et al. 2018 | moderate | 9% BadNets / 66% Blended | Partial |

Our implementation reproduces the core results from each foundational paper. The partial
match on Fine Pruning reflects a known limitation: Liu et al. evaluated pruning on VGG-style
networks with more redundant neurons than our compact LeNet-5. The defence has less to prune
and therefore less room to disrupt backdoor neurons without harming clean accuracy.

### Comparison Against State-of-the-Art Defences

**Fine Pruning (Liu et al. 2018)**
Performs best of the four defences overall. 65.6 pp ASR reduction against Blended with
only 0.41% CA drop is a strong result. Performance against BadNets (9 pp reduction) is
weak, consistent with Liu et al.'s observation that patch triggers occupy neurons that also
encode legitimate features, making them resistant to pruning without CA cost.

**Activation Clustering (Chen et al. 2019)**
Returned identical CA and ASR values to Spectral Signatures across both attacks, suggesting
neither defence detected any poisoned samples and both returned the model unchanged. This
is consistent with their documented failure mode: when the trigger is sufficiently strong
(83-99% ASR), poisoned and clean samples may cluster together in activation space and cannot
be separated by k-means or singular value decomposition alone.

**Spectral Signatures (Tran et al. 2018)**
Same outcome as Activation Clustering. The spectral anomaly score assumes poisoned samples
are outliers in the covariance spectrum of penultimate-layer activations. Strong triggers
that are pervasive across many samples (Blended, 10% poison) may not appear as outliers
relative to the clean distribution, explaining the failure to remove them.

**Neural Cleanse (Wang et al. 2019)**
Underperformed relative to its theoretical capability. The reverse-engineered trigger
optimisation was capped at 500 steps due to CPU constraints. Wang et al. typically use
1000+ steps on GPU. The resulting trigger estimate was insufficiently accurate to neutralise
the backdoor via unlearning. Extended optimisation would improve ASR reduction.

**Advanced TSR-Specific Attacks (Wu et al. 2025, 2026)**
FlipBAT (Wu et al. 2026) uses a self-flipping endogenous strategy that generates triggers
from internal model gradients, making them harder to detect with activation-space methods.
Nightfall Deception (Wu et al. 2025) exploits low-light image conditions specific to
traffic sign capture. Our Label Consistent implementation is in the same family (stealthy
no-label-flip attack) but failed under current hyperparameters (epsilon=8/255, 10 PGD
steps). These more advanced attacks require larger epsilon budgets, more PGD iterations,
and potentially higher poison rates than standard label-flip attacks. This gap highlights
that robust stealthy attacks remain an open challenge beyond simple PGD perturbation.

### Summary of Attack-Defence Effectiveness

| Attack | Easiest to Defend Against | Hardest to Defend Against |
|---|---|---|
| BadNets | None of the tested defences are fully effective | Fine Pruning (partial, 74% ASR remaining) |
| Blended | All four defences achieve > 55 pp ASR reduction | Fine Pruning is most effective (34% ASR remaining) |
| Label Consistent | n/a (attack failed to embed backdoor) | n/a |

The central finding is that no single lightweight defence fully neutralises BadNets while
preserving CA. The stealthy patch trigger exploits neurons shared with clean classification
pathways, making surgical removal difficult without retraining. Blended, despite being
the stronger attack at 99.7% ASR, is paradoxically more susceptible to defences because
its distributed trigger signature is encoded in specific dormant neurons that can be pruned
or clustered out without damaging clean accuracy paths.
